# Paper: Seasonal and diurnal variations of ambient PM2.5 concentration in urban and rural environments in Beijing

## Citation
- **Authors**: Xiujuan Zhao, Xiaoling Zhang, Xiaofeng Xu, Jing Xu, Wei Meng, Weiwei Pu
- **Year**: 2009
- **Journal**: Atmospheric Environment, Volume 43, Issue 18, Pages 2893-2900
- **DOI**: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.atmosenv.2009.03.009
- **Tier**: Tier-1 (Peer-reviewed journal)

## Key Findings

### Diurnal Patterns - Urban Area

The paper identifies a **bimodal diurnal pattern** in urban PM2.5 concentrations with two distinct peaks:

**Morning Peak (7:00-8:00 AM)**: Attributed to enhanced anthropogenic activity during rush hours. Traffic emissions combine with stable morning boundary layer conditions.

**Evening Peak (7:00-11:00 PM)**: This is the highest PM2.5 concentration period. The mechanism involves three factors working together: (1) decreases of boundary layer height, (2) reduced wind speed in the afternoon, and (3) increased source activity during the afternoon rush hour.

**Minimum (around noon)**: The lowest PM2.5 concentrations occur around noon when the boundary layer is most developed and vertical mixing is strongest.

### Diurnal Patterns - Rural Area

The rural area shows a **unimodal pattern** with a single significant peak between 5:00 and 11:00 PM. This is attributed to southerly winds carrying pollutants from southern urban areas northward.

### Seasonal Patterns - Urban Area

**Winter**: Highest PM2.5 concentrations due to heating emissions and stable atmospheric conditions with low boundary layer heights.

**Summer**: Lowest PM2.5 concentrations due to increased precipitation (wet deposition), higher boundary layer heights, and stronger vertical mixing.

### Seasonal Patterns - Rural Area

**Spring**: Maximum PM2.5 concentration, influenced by dust events from Gobi desert.

**Summer**: Second maximum, attributed to regional transport under southerly wind conditions.

**Winter**: Minimum PM2.5 concentration in rural areas.

### Key Mechanisms Identified

1. **Boundary Layer Height (BLH)**: Diurnal and seasonal variability of BLH is a dominant factor controlling PM2.5 concentrations. Lower BLH leads to higher PM2.5 due to reduced dilution volume.

2. **Wind Speed**: Lower wind speeds reduce horizontal dispersion and allow pollutant accumulation.

3. **Wind Direction**: Southerly winds transport urban pollutants to rural areas, significantly enhancing rural PM2.5 levels.

4. **Rainfall**: Year-to-year variability of rainfall has important influence on seasonal variation through wet deposition.

5. **Source Emissions**: Rush hour traffic emissions create morning and evening peaks in urban areas.

## Quantitative Data

- Urban PM2.5 concentrations: 79-138 μg/m³ with significant seasonal variation
- Annual average PM2.5 in Beijing (2000): ~101 μg/m³
- Regional contribution: During southerly wind flow, about 70% of PM2.5 can be attributed to sources outside Beijing
